# How the Human Brain Receives Knowledge: Research for Aily's Design

**Date:** 2026-04-08
**Purpose:** Understand neuroscience of learning to design Feishu → Zettelkasten → Brain workflow

---

## Executive Summary

The human brain doesn't store knowledge like a hard drive—it **reconstructs** memories through dynamic neural networks. For Aily to effectively feed your Zettelkasten into your brain, we must align with how the brain actually encodes, consolidates, and retrieves information.

**Key insight:** Your Feishu captures are the raw input. The Zettelkasten is the externalized memory system. But for knowledge to become *permanent*, it must go through the same processes the brain uses: encoding → elaboration → consolidation → active recall.

---

## Phase 1: Memory Formation (The First 24 Hours)

### Encoding: From Sensation to Neural Pattern

When you read something in Feishu or save a link to Aily, your brain begins encoding:

1. **Sensory Memory** (milliseconds): Visual/auditory input briefly held
2. **Working Memory** (seconds): Information actively processed (~4-7 chunks)
3. **Hippocampal Encoding** (minutes): The hippocampus converts experience into neural patterns

**Critical finding:** [Ripple activity in the hippocampus](https://www.nature.com/subjects/consolidation/ncomms) tracks key moments during experiences and predicts what you'll later remember. Sharp-wave ripples (SWRs)—periods of highly synchronous neural firing—are the brain's save button.

**For Aily:** This means not everything you capture matters. The brain prioritizes:
- Novel information (violates predictions)
- Emotionally salient content
- Information connected to existing knowledge

*Design implication:* Aily should help users identify what's actually novel vs. redundant.

### Long-Term Potentiation (LTP): The Cellular Basis of Memory

At the synaptic level, [memory formation follows LTP](https://www.hilarispublisher.com/open-access/decoding-the-molecular-mechanisms-behind-memory-formation-in-the-hippocampus.pdf):

1. **NMDA receptor activation** → Calcium influx
2. **Protein kinases (PKC, CaMKII)** activated
3. **CREB transcription factor** triggers gene expression
4. **New proteins synthesized** to strengthen synapses
5. **Structural changes** make synapses permanently stronger

**Key insight:** This takes time. The initial encoding is fragile—easily disrupted for hours after learning.

## Phase 2: Consolidation (Making Memories Permanent)

### Synaptic Consolidation (Hours)

Immediately after encoding, the brain begins [synaptic consolidation](https://remittancesreview.com/menu-script/index.php/remittances/article/download/2078/1514/4531):
- Activity-dependent protein synthesis
- Epigenetic modifications (DNA methylation, histone acetylation)
- Glial cells (microglia, astrocytes) actively participate in plasticity

**For Aily:** Notes captured to Obsidian immediately after reading are in this fragile state. They need processing to become permanent.

### Systems Consolidation (Days to Years)

The [hippocampus gradually transfers memories](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/4c83/8aca04fb74ec9bad1bca7b910ac652b40f8d.pdf) to neocortical networks:

1. **Initial storage:** Hippocampus holds the memory index
2. **Sleep reactivation:** During slow-wave sleep, hippocampal sharp-wave ripples replay experiences
3. **Cortical integration:** Repeated replay strengthens neocortical connections
4. **Hippocampal independence:** Eventually, memories can be accessed without hippocampus

**[Targeted Memory Reactivation research](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-025-00299-1)** shows: Reactivating memories during sleep at moments of high neuronal excitability (UP phases) enhances consolidation.

**For Aily:** This is why spaced repetition works—you're triggering reactivation at optimal intervals.

### The Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve

[Hermann Ebbinghaus (1885)](https://memoryos.com/article/the-ebbinghaus-forgetting-curve-and-how-to-hack-it) established that memory follows exponential decay:

| Time After Learning | Retention |
|---------------------|-----------|
| 20 minutes | ~50% |
| 24 hours | ~30% |
| 31 days | ~10% |

**Mathematical formula:** R = e^(-t/S)

**Critical insight:** Each review resets the curve, making it shallower. After 5-7 well-timed reviews, retention extends from days to months or years.

## Phase 3: Elaborative Encoding (Connecting to Existing Knowledge)

### Schema Theory and Knowledge Networks

The brain stores information in [semantic networks and schemas](https://helpfulprofessor.com/elaborative-encoding/) (cognitive blueprints). New information is integrated into existing mental frameworks rather than remaining isolated.

**[Elaborative encoding](https://psychology.tips/elaborative-encoding/)** is the process of actively connecting new information to prior knowledge:

> "Actively relating new information to knowledge that is already in memory" — Bedford, 2020

### Mechanisms of Elaborative Encoding

1. **Multiple Retrieval Pathways:** Each connection creates a potential access route. An isolated fact has one entry point; a fact connected to five concepts has six retrieval routes.

2. **Richness and Distinctiveness:**
   - Richness = high number of interconnections
   - Distinctiveness = clear differentiation from similar items

3. **Self-Reference Effect:** Connecting to personal experiences creates strongest encoding because it links to your most detailed knowledge structure—your own life.

4. **Reduced Cognitive Load:** Connecting to existing schemas leverages organized long-term memory structures.

### Levels of Processing Framework

| Encoding Level | Characteristics | Memory Outcome |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Shallow** (Structural) | Surface features, repetition | Weak, quickly forgotten |
| **Deep** (Semantic) | Meaning and relationships | Strong, durable |
| **Elaborative** | Active connection-building | Very strong; multiple pathways |

**For Aily:** This is why atomic Zettelkasten notes that link to existing notes are so effective—they force elaborative encoding.

## Phase 4: Active Recall (Strengthening Through Retrieval)

### The Testing Effect

[Active recall](https://sidetrackedday.com/blog/active-recall-study-method) (retrieval practice) is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology. Every time you successfully retrieve a memory, it becomes easier to access in the future.

**Key study:** [Roediger & Karpicke (2006)](http://psychnet.wustl.edu/memory/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/Roediger-Karpicke-2006_PPS.pdf)
- Study-test group: Study once, take recall test
- Study-study group: Study twice

**Results after 1 week:** Study-test group significantly outperformed study-study group, despite less exposure to material.

### Neural Mechanisms

[Neuroimaging research](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2020.04.02.020321.full.pdf) shows retrieval practice:
- Activates hippocampus and prefrontal cortex together
- Strengthens neural connections through reconstruction
- Gradually shifts memories from hippocampal to neocortical storage
- Increases distinctiveness in posterior parietal cortex

### Effectiveness Statistics

| Method | Retention After 1 Week |
|--------|------------------------|
| Passive review (rereading) | ~40% |
| Active recall | ~80% |
| Active recall + spaced repetition | ~90%+ |

**[Research consensus](https://www.structural-learning.com/post/testing-effect-retrieval-practice):** Retrieval practice improves retention by 50-100% over passive methods.

### Desirable Difficulty

Retrieval practice feels harder than rereading—and that's why it works. [Cognitive effort during learning strengthens encoding](https://cognitivetrain.com/elaborative-encoding/). The struggle to recall, then checking and correcting, produces stronger memory than effortless review.

**Error-correction is particularly effective:** Getting it wrong, then learning the correct answer strengthens memory more than simply reviewing known material.

## The Zettelkasten Method as External Brain

### Neuroscience Parallels

The [Zettelkasten method](https://www.obsibrain.com/blog/zettelkasten-how-to-build-a-connected-second-brain-that-actually-grows-with-you) explicitly mimics how the brain stores information:

> "This system works like a network of neurons mimicking the human brain and neuronal connections: this is why it is also referred to as 'second brain'" — Academic research

| Brain Mechanism | Zettelkasten Equivalent |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| Neurons | Atomic notes (one idea per note) |
| Synapses | Bidirectional links between notes |
| Neural networks | Knowledge webs emerging from links |
| Pattern recognition | Serendipitous connection discovery |
| Plasticity | Dynamic network evolution |

### Key Principles

1. **Atomic Notes:** Each note contains exactly one idea that can stand alone—like a neuron with specific receptive fields.

2. **Connected Networks:** Notes link bidirectionally, creating webs rather than silos—like neural networks rather than hierarchical folders.

3. **Emergence:** New patterns surface from the network structure—like insight formation in the brain.

4. **Threshold Effect:** [30-50 well-linked notes](https://affine.pro/blog/zettelkasten-method) begin generating insights; 200-300 create dense networks with regular serendipitous discoveries.

## Implications for Aily's Design

### Current Workflow

```
Feishu (capture) → Aily (process) → Obsidian (Zettelkasten) → Your Brain
```

### Recommended Enhancements Based on Brain Science

#### 1. Capture → Encoding Stage

**What the brain needs:** Novelty detection, initial attention

**Aily enhancement:**
- **Deduplication** (already implemented via URL hash): Prevents redundant encoding
- **Source quality scoring:** Flag high-quality sources (like high-salience stimuli)
- **Emotional/conceptual tags:** Help the brain prioritize

#### 2. Processing → Elaboration Stage

**What the brain needs:** Connection to existing knowledge

**Aily enhancements:**
- **Atomic note generation:** Break captures into single ideas (atomic notes)
- **Suggested connections:** AI suggests links to existing notes (like elaborative encoding)
- **Self-reference prompts:** "How does this connect to your work/experience?"
- **Concept extraction:** Identify key concepts for linking

#### 3. Storage → Consolidation Stage

**What the brain needs:** Spaced repetition, sleep

**Aily enhancements:**
- **Daily digest scheduling** (already implemented): Regular review at optimal intervals
- **Spaced repetition integration:** Queue notes for review at 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 21 days
- **Sleep-aware timing:** Schedule digests before sleep to leverage consolidation

#### 4. Retrieval → Active Recall Stage

**What the brain needs:** Testing, error-correction

**Aily enhancements:**
- **Active recall questions:** Generate questions from notes (not just summaries)
- **Cloze deletion:** Fill-in-the-blank prompts from key concepts
- **Verification layer** (already implemented): Forces "checking" behavior like error-correction
- **Retrieval practice notifications:** "Can you explain [concept] without looking?"

### Proposed: The Aily Learning Loop

```
1. CAPTURE (Feishu)
   ↓
2. PROCESS (Aily → Atomic notes + Suggested links)
   ↓
3. ELABORATE (You → Accept/modify links, add personal connections)
   ↓
4. STORE (Obsidian Zettelkasten)
   ↓
5. CONSOLIDATE (Spaced repetition reminders)
   ↓
6. RECALL (Active retrieval questions)
   ↓
7. FEEDBACK (Verification + Error correction)
   ↓
   Back to 4 (notes updated with new connections)
```

### Critical Implementation Points

| Brain Requirement | Aily Feature | Priority |
|-------------------|--------------|----------|
| Elaborative encoding | Auto-suggest note connections | P1 |
| Spaced repetition | Review queue with intervals | P1 |
| Active recall | Generate questions from notes | P2 |

## Case Study: Andrej Karpathy's LLM Knowledge Base

Andrej Karpathy ([tweet](https://x.com/karpathy/status/2039805659525644595)) uses a workflow that validates our brain-aligned design. His system demonstrates how LLM + Obsidian can mirror the brain's memory processes:

### Karpathy's Architecture

```
Raw Data (raw/)
    ↓ LLM "compiles"
Compiled Wiki (.md files with structure)
    ↓ Obsidian as IDE
Human queries and explores
    ↓ LLM outputs
New markdown/slides/visualizations
    ↓ Feed back
Enhanced Wiki
```

### Key Principles

**1. Raw vs. Compiled Distinction**
- `raw/` directory: Source documents (articles, papers, repos, images)
- Wiki directory: LLM-processed, linked, categorized knowledge
- *Brain parallel:* Sensory memory → Consolidated long-term memory

**2. LLM-Maintained Wiki**
- Karpathy: "I rarely touch it directly, it's the domain of the LLM"
- LLM writes summaries, categorizes, finds connections, suggests articles
- *Brain parallel:* Elaborative encoding and schema building automated

**3. Accumulating Queries**
- Outputs get "filed back into the wiki to enhance it for further queries"
- Explorations "add up" in the knowledge base
- *Brain parallel:* Each retrieval strengthens the memory trace

**4. No Fancy RAG Required**
- At ~100 articles, ~400K words, LLM can read all relevant data
- Auto-maintained index files help navigation
- *Brain parallel:* Working memory can hold 4-7 chunks; the wiki structure creates scaffolds

**5. Linting/Health Checks**
- LLM checks for: inconsistent data, missing data, interesting connections
- Suggests new article candidates
- *Brain parallel:* Similar to our verification layer—checking for accuracy

### Tools Karpathy Uses

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Obsidian Web Clipper | Capture web articles to `raw/` |
| Hotkey for image download | Local images for LLM reference |
| Obsidian | IDE frontend for viewing data |
| Marp plugin | Slide rendering |
| Custom CLI tools | Search engine for LLM queries |

### What This Means for Aily

Karpathy's system validates our research but adds crucial implementation details:

| Brain Science | Aily Implementation | Karpathy's Approach |
|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Encoding | Feishu webhook capture | Obsidian Web Clipper |
| Elaboration | Connection suggester | LLM auto-compiles wiki |
| Consolidation | Spaced repetition | Accumulating queries |
| Retrieval | Active recall | Q&A with LLM agent |

**Key insight:** The separation between **raw capture** and **compiled knowledge** is essential. Raw is messy; compiled is structured. The LLM bridges them.

## Conclusion

The brain receives knowledge through a multi-phase process: **encoding → elaboration → consolidation → retrieval**. Each phase has specific requirements that Aily can support.

**Current Aily aligns with:**
- Capture (Feishu webhook) ✓
- Initial processing (parser pipeline) ✓
- Storage (Obsidian writer) ✓
- Verification (claim checking) ✓

**Key additions needed (validated by both neuroscience AND Karpathy's workflow):**
1. **Raw/Compiled separation** - `Aily Drafts/` = raw, compiled wiki = structured
2. **LLM-maintained wiki** - Atomic notes auto-generated and linked
3. **Accumulating queries** - Q&A outputs feed back into knowledge base
4. **Health check/linting** - Automated verification of consistency

**Karpathy's validation:** "I think there is room here for an incredible new product instead of a hacky collection of scripts."

The goal: Your Feishu captures become a **living knowledge base** that compounds over time—just like Karpathy's system, but automated from your IM messages.
